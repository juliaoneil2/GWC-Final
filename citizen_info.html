Citizenship Process

U.S. Citizenship

To become a citizen at birth, you must:

  • Have been born in the United States or certain territories or outlying possessions of the United States, and subject to the jurisdiction of the United States; OR
  • Had a parent or parents who were citizens at the time of your birth (if you were born abroad) and meet other requirements

    • To become a citizen after birth, you must:

  • Apply for “derived” or “acquired” citizenship through parents
  • Apply for naturalization

    • The Naturalization Test

    In order to take the Naturalization Interview, you must fill up Form N-400, also known as Application of Naturalization. Most naturalization applicants are required to take a test on English and Civics (U.S. history and government). For this specific application, a filing fee is required, and currently it costs $725. If you are unable to pay the fees for any reason, USCIS provides feewaivers. For fee processing and/or fee waiver requirements, check this link: Forms and Fees. For more info on the Naturalization Test, check this website: Naturalization Test.

    These are the documents you will need to bring to the Naturalization Interview:

  • Your Green Card (formally known as Form I-551, Permanent Resident Card)
  • A photocopy of the front and back of your Green Card
  • Your driver’s license or state-issued identification card
  • All current and expired passports or travel documents
  • Copies of your tax returns for the past five years (or three years if you are married to a U.S. citizen)

    • However, depending on your individual case, you may need to submit other items with your application. For additional info, check this website: Apply for Citizenship.

    Dual Citizenship

    Dual citizenship — or dual nationality — means being a citizen of two countries simultaneously and sharing the rights and responsibilities of the citizens in each country. The U.S. Government does allow dual citizenship, however it doesn't necessarily mean the country you are from does, too. It’s therefore essential to understand the dual citizenship rules in your country of origin before pursuing U.S. citizenship. To learn more about dual citizenship, check this link: Dual Citizenship - Rights and Responsibilities.
